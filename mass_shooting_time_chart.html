Serious Facts of Mass Shootings in the U.S

The death rate in the United States due to gun violence is higher than that of any other high-income country (Grinshteyn and Hemenway, 2016). In the United States, firearm-related injuries are now the leading cause of death in children and adolescents (Goldstick et al., 2022). There is a dramatically increasing trend of mass shootings in recent years. In the year of 2024, there have already been more than 470 mass shootings in the United States, leaving more than 475 people dead and 2,050 injured. Besides, the geographical distribution of mass shootings is also unbalanced, data amassed from 1966 to 2016 show that the western and southern regions of the country have historically seen two-thirds of the mass shootings in the nation. The related factors to mass shootings, such as unemployment rate, economic status and local policies, need to be further analyzed to develop appropriate methods.

Mass shooting is defined by the FBI as an event in which one or more individuals are “actively engaged in killing or attempting to kill people in a populated area. Implicit in this definition is the shooter's use of a firearm.” And the Gun Violence Archive defines a mass shooting as a shooting that injured or killed four or more people, not including the shooter. Therefore, we conclude the definition of mass shooting as “an event in which one or more individuals are engaged in killing and 4 or more victims are killed”.

Data analysis is of great importance in the research of mass shooting events. Visualizations tools can be leveraged to reveal the time trend, geographical feature, and event related factors to the rate of mass shootings which are essential for political policy, public safety and social awareness. Addressing this crisis demands collaborative efforts across different domains, with data serving as a cornerstone for informed decision-making and effective action.

Time Trend of Mass Shooting Incidents in the U.S

The ten-year trend of mass shootings from 2014-2023 is visualized with the data from the Gun Violence Archive. The average monthly number of mass shootings is 39 in the period, with a noticeable increasing trend after 2020. This coincides with the Covid-19 pandemic period, which increased peoples' pressure and caused social instability. There is great evidence to confirm the overall rise in gun violence during the pandemic. Between 2019 and 2020, the overall firearm homicide rate increased about 35%, according to the CDC's Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report. Between March 2020 and February 2021, there were about 62,500 firearm-related incidents in the US, which resulted in more than 10,000 excess non-fatal injuries and about 4,400 excess deaths. The monthly number of mass shootings during the pandemic period (03/2020 - 04/2023 ), also rose to 55, which is higher than the trend of previous years. Among all months, the peak number of mass shootings occurred in June 2020. 94 incidents happened during that month, probably due to the accumulation of pressure caused by the pandemic.

When it comes to the number of victims killed and injured, the pandemic also shows strong influence, with an obvious increasing trend in the number of victims during that period. However, the most serious event happened during October 2017, with 532 victims injured and 88 victims killed, much higher than the other months. The reason for the irregular stat is probably due to the 2017 Las Vegas Shooting, which was the deadliest mass shooting by a lone gunman in American history. During Route 91 Harvest music festival on October 1st. 64-year-old Stephen Paddock fired into the crowd of concert goers from his 32nd-floor suite, killing 60 people and injuring over 413. Paddock used a stockpile of firearms, many equipped with bump stocks, to carry out the attack. The motive remains unclear. The massacre sparked national debates on gun control, particularly on the regulation of bump stocks and semi-automatic weapons, and underscored the urgent need for improved public safety measures.
